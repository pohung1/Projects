Effect of Training

  • train = 0.23352

  • p = 0.029985

  • Percent effect ~= 100 * 0.2335 = 23.35%

    • e^(0.2335) - 1 = 26.3%

  • Participation in the training program increased 1978 earnings by approximately 23-26 percent, holding education, race, and prior earnings constant. This effect is statistically significant at the 5% level.

Black Coefficient

  • black = -0.57754

  • p < 0.001

    • e^(-0.5775) - 1 ~= - 44%

  • Black participants earned roughly 40-45 percent less than non-Black participants, conditional on other covariates.

Education

  • educ = 0.0358

  • p = 0.228

  • each additional year of education ~= 3.6% higher earnings, but not statistically significant.

Summary

  • The log model is has a better performance because it shows a large percentage effect and this is randomize data, it has causal interpretation.

  • The log-earnings regression indicates that participation in the job training program increased 1978 earnings by approximately 23-26 percent, holding education, race, and prior earnings constant. This effect is statistically significant at the 5 percent level. black participants earned roughly 40-45 percent less than non-Black participants, conditional on covariates. Prior earnings from 1975 are marginally predictive of 1978 earnings, while 1974 earnings are not statistically significant. Overall, the results suggest that the training program has a sizable and economically meaningful impact on earnings.

Is There a Omitted Variable Bias (OVB) in the Model?

Is OVB if:

  1. It affects the dependent variable (which is earnings)
  2. It is correlated with the train variable

There is not OVB if:

  1. either condition above fails –> that means there are no bias in the train coefficient.
